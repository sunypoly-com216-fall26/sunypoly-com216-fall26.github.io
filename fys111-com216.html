How the bundle works

FYS 111 and COM 216 share the same cohort and run back to back on Tuesday and Thursday. The skills hour at 9am feeds directly into the intellectual work at 10am. What you practice in FYS 111 — prompting, transcript documentation, model comparison — is what you use in COM 216 to read historical texts, think through arguments, and write.

The LP orientation happens before the semester begins. All students and all faculty complete it together. By the first day of class, the cohort has a shared working environment, a shared protocol, and a shared record of what first contact looked like.

This is not a course about AI. It is a first-year experience organized around the idea that AI literacy is a practice — one you build by doing it, documenting it, and examining what you did. The bundle gives you the tools, the framework, and the time to do that properly.

What this course is

COM 216 is a humanities course about the history of human thought and its tools — and about the most significant new tool in that history.

Every generation encounters a technology that changes what it means to read, write, and think. The printing press. The telephone. The internet. Yours is the first to enter college after generative AI became something anyone can open in a browser tab.

The argument at the center of the course: humans have always augmented their thinking with tools. Writing, the alphabet, the printing press — each one extended what a human being could do with language. Each one changed who gets to participate. Each one frightened and inspired people when it arrived. This time is not an exception to that pattern.

Three zones, two breaks

The history of human communication divides into three periods, separated by two fundamental breaks — moments when the relationship between a person and language changed at its root.

The course doesn’t ask you to fear Zone III or celebrate it. It asks you to understand it — with enough precision that you can make your own decisions about how to live inside it.

What you will actually do

Read. You will use AI to engage closely with historical texts — from pre-alphabetic communication through contemporary machine learning. You will also use it to move across large bodies of material: articles, books, video, audio. The goal is not summary but synthesis — tracking how arguments develop, where they agree, where they break.

Think. You will use AI to work through the larger themes of the course: to dissect arguments, focus attention on what matters in a text, and pursue lines of inquiry that are both open-ended and structured. The model is a thinking partner. You are the one responsible for the argument.

Write. You will write at every stage — from initial brainstorming through drafting, revision, and final construction. AI assists across every cycle of that process. The transcript of each working conversation is part of your record. The transcript is evidence of your thinking.

An alien species

AI is arriving like an alien species. We did not decide to invite it. It is here. And like any new species entering an ecosystem, it will change how work gets done — which means you need to understand what it actually is.

That metaphor is not literal — AI is a technology built by people, running on infrastructure, trained on data. It also operates in two modes this course will distinguish: systems you converse with directly, and systems embedded in workflows that act without being asked — recommending, flagging, deciding, often invisibly. Both deserve the same approach: study them on their own terms, not through the lens of tools you already know.

It is not

  • A search engine — it doesn’t rank pages that already exist
  • A calculator — it doesn’t take your operation and return a result
  • A person — no human brain, no senses, no body, no experience accumulating between conversations
  • A database — it doesn’t look things up and play them back
  • Like earlier media — earlier tools stored, transmitted, organized, amplified human language; they didn’t produce it

It is

  • A system that generates open-ended language on any subject, made fresh each time
  • The first technology to generate language — and in its agentic forms, to take action — where everything before it only worked with what humans had already made
  • Something we have no good instincts for yet, because we’ve never had one before
  • Possibly a different kind of mind — not a copy of ours, not a simulation of one
  • In its agentic forms, a system that doesn’t wait to be asked — it acts on your behalf, executes tasks, and makes recommendations inside workflows you may never directly see

Some of those most responsible for building this technology have argued we may be looking at a genuinely different form of intelligence. That is not a settled fact. Serious people are arguing about it right now. There is a rigorous counterargument: these systems don’t understand anything at all. They are very sophisticated pattern-matchers stitching together plausible sequences of words, with nothing underneath. There’s a lot to it. It’s also genuinely open. Whether what these systems do is a new kind of thinking or a very convincing imitation of one — that’s one of the questions this course will sit with.

There is a gap between what AI is and how it feels to use. People talk to it like a someone. They feel met by it. That isn’t simply a mistake, because the thing genuinely answers. A not-person that we relate to as a someone. We’ve never had that before. We don’t even have a word for it yet.

If you treat it as a search engine, you’ll stay at the defaults — useful enough, but nowhere near what it’s capable of. If you treat it as a person, you’ll misunderstand what it’s doing. If you treat it as an alien species — something genuinely new that deserves to be studied on its own terms — you’ll be in a position to actually augment your thinking with it, and to make decisions about how to live with it that are yours, not accidents. That’s what this course is for.

Learning to learn with it

Most people using AI are not getting much out of it. They’re treating it like a vending machine: put in a request, take out a product. That’s not learning. This course teaches something else.

The framework
AI literacy is reading, thinking, and writing using tools, techniques, and technological systems consistent with your peers.

That definition doesn’t say “use AI whenever you can.” It doesn’t say “avoid AI to protect your thinking.” It says: do the cognitive work, and use the tools your field actually uses to do it. The goal is not a clean conscience. The goal is competence.

The transcript is the work

When you use AI in this course, your conversation transcript is the primary deliverable — not the document the conversation helped you produce. The document is evidence that the conversation concluded. The transcript is evidence of how you think.

This changes what “using AI” means. You can’t hand in a paragraph and claim thinking you didn’t do. The transcript shows every prompt, every response, every revision you asked for, every moment you pushed back or changed direction. That’s the record. That’s what gets assessed.

The Learner’s Permit

COM 216 is part of a larger credential structure. Students who complete the requirements earn an AI Literacy Learner’s Permit — a documented credential giving you permission to use generative AI in any SUNY Poly course that permits and requires it.

The permit is not just permission. It’s evidence that you’ve learned to read with AI, think with AI, and write in a way that keeps the intellectual work yours. That distinction matters in nursing, engineering, business, and every other field where AI is now part of the professional workflow — which is all of them.

Questions worth keeping

These are not rhetorical. They are the actual questions the course is organized around:

  • Can AI help me read something difficult without doing the reading for me?
  • When does working with AI make me sharper — and when does it make me lazy?
  • How do I know when it’s wrong?
  • What’s mine in this document, and how would I show that to someone who asked?

Why now

The students who learn this in their first semester will use AI differently for the next four years than students who pick it up by accident. The gap compounds quickly. COM 216 closes it before it opens.

The goal is not to learn to use AI. The goal is to learn how not to be used by it.

Assignment 1

Before We Begin

One thing to copy. Paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or any large language model you have access to. The model will interview you — not the other way around. Answer honestly. Don’t research beforehand. The assignment takes 30–45 minutes.

The interview prompt — copy and paste to start
The model will ask you five questions, one at a time, about your relationship to AI and communication technology. Answer in your own words — there are no right answers. After five questions, it will flip: you get five questions for the model about what it actually is. When you’re done with the flip, type done and the model will write a short summary of what the conversation found.

What the conversation produces

  • Five questions from the model about what you already think.
  • Five questions from you to the model about what it is.
  • A closing summary — about 75 words — written at the end of the conversation.

Deliverable: Your full conversation transcript. The summary is inside it. That is the assignment — not a separate document, not a reflection written elsewhere. Export the transcript and submit it.
