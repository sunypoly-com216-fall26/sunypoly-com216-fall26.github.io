## Learning to learn with it

> Most people using AI are not getting much out of it. They're treating it like a vending machine: put in a request, take out a product. That's not learning. This course teaches something else.

**The framework**

> AI literacy is reading, thinking, and writing using tools, techniques, and technological systems consistent with your peers.

That definition doesn't say "use AI whenever you can." It doesn't say "avoid AI to protect your thinking." It says: **do the cognitive work**, and use the tools your field actually uses to do it. The goal is not a clean conscience. The goal is competence.

### The transcript is the work

When you use AI in this course, your conversation transcript is the primary deliverable — not the document the conversation helped you produce. The document is evidence that the conversation concluded. The transcript is evidence of how you think.

This changes what "using AI" means. You can't hand in a paragraph and claim thinking you didn't do. The transcript shows every prompt, every response, every revision you asked for, every moment you pushed back or changed direction. That's the record. That's what gets assessed.

### The Learner's Permit

COM 216 is part of a larger credential structure. Students who complete the requirements earn an **AI Literacy Learner's Permit** — a documented credential giving you permission to use generative AI in any SUNY Poly course that permits and requires it.

The permit is not just permission. It's evidence that you've learned to read with AI, think with AI, and write in a way that keeps the intellectual work yours. That distinction matters in nursing, engineering, business, and every other field where AI is now part of the professional workflow — which is all of them.

### Questions worth keeping

These are not rhetorical. They are the actual questions the course is organized around:

- Can AI help me read something difficult without doing the reading for me?
- When does working with AI make me sharper — and when does it make me lazy?
- How do I know when it's wrong?
- What's mine in this document, and how would I show that to someone who asked?

---

### Why now

The students who learn this in their first semester will use AI differently for the next four years than students who pick it up by accident. The gap compounds quickly. COM 216 closes it before it opens.

The goal is not to learn to use AI. The goal is to learn how not to be used by it.
