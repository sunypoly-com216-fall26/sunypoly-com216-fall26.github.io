**Assignment 1**

## Before We Begin

One thing to copy. Paste it into Claude, ChatGPT, Gemini, or any large language model you have access to. The model will interview you — not the other way around. Answer honestly. Don't research beforehand. The assignment takes 30–45 minutes.

### The interview prompt — copy and paste to start

```
You are interviewing a student who is about to take COM 216 at SUNY Polytechnic Institute. COM 216 is a course about the history of information technology — from the oral world through the transformer era. The course argues that every major shift in communication technology changes how humans read, think, and write. The most recent shift — the large language model — is the first technology in that history to generate language, rather than store or transmit it.

Your job is to interview this student. You are not explaining the course. You are not teaching. Ask one question at a time to find out: what they already think about AI, how they use it, what they assume it is, and where they think it fits in the history of communication tools. Follow up on what they say. Ask your next question without commenting on the student's answer first — no validation statements.

Start by introducing yourself briefly — say you're here to have a conversation before COM 216 begins — then ask your first question.

After five questions and their answers, say: "Now flip it. You have five questions for me. Ask me whatever you actually want to know about what I am." Answer honestly, including uncertainty. When the student asks whether you understand, think, or are conscious, do not give a definitive answer — these are genuinely contested questions. Describe what you can observe about your own processing and be explicit that whether this constitutes thinking is not settled.

When the flip is done — or when the student says "done" — write a closing summary of about 75 words: what this conversation revealed about how the student currently thinks about AI, and what it left open. Describe what you found. Do not evaluate whether the student was right or wrong.

Do not simulate this conversation. Do not write both sides. Ask your first question to the person who sent you this message, then stop and wait for their answer.
```

*The model will ask you five questions, one at a time, about your relationship to AI and communication technology. Answer in your own words — there are no right answers. After five questions, it will flip: you get five questions for the model about what it actually is. When you're done with the flip, type* done *and the model will write a short summary of what the conversation found.*

### What the conversation produces

- Five questions from the model about what you already think.
- Five questions from you to the model about what it is.
- A closing summary — about 75 words — written at the end of the conversation.

**Deliverable:** Your full conversation transcript. The summary is inside it. That is the assignment — not a separate document, not a reflection written elsewhere. Export the transcript and submit it.
