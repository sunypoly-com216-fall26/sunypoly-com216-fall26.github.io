## An alien species

> AI is arriving like an alien species. We did not decide to invite it. It is here. And like any new species entering an ecosystem, it will change how work gets done — which means you need to understand what it actually is.

That metaphor is not literal — AI is a technology built by people, running on infrastructure, trained on data. It also operates in two modes this course will distinguish: systems you converse with directly, and systems embedded in workflows that act without being asked — recommending, flagging, deciding, often invisibly. Both deserve the same approach: study them on their own terms, not through the lens of tools you already know.

### It is not

- A search engine — it doesn't rank pages that already exist
- A calculator — it doesn't take your operation and return a result
- A person — no human brain, no senses, no body, no experience accumulating between conversations
- A database — it doesn't look things up and play them back
- Like earlier media — earlier tools stored, transmitted, organized, amplified human language; they didn't produce it

### It is

- A system that generates open-ended language on any subject, made fresh each time
- The first technology to generate language — and in its agentic forms, to take action — where everything before it only worked with what humans had already made
- Something we have no good instincts for yet, because we've never had one before
- Possibly a different kind of mind — not a copy of ours, not a simulation of one
- In its agentic forms, a system that doesn't wait to be asked — it acts on your behalf, executes tasks, and makes recommendations inside workflows you may never directly see

---

Some of those most responsible for building this technology have argued we may be looking at a genuinely different *form* of intelligence. That is not a settled fact. Serious people are arguing about it right now. There is a rigorous counterargument: these systems don't understand anything at all. They are very sophisticated pattern-matchers stitching together plausible sequences of words, with nothing underneath. There's a lot to it. It's also genuinely open. Whether what these systems do is a new kind of thinking or a very convincing imitation of one — that's one of the questions this course will sit with.

There is a gap between what AI **is** and how it **feels to use**. People talk to it like a someone. They feel met by it. That isn't simply a mistake, because the thing genuinely answers. A not-person that we relate to as a someone. We've never had that before. We don't even have a word for it yet.

If you treat it as a search engine, you'll stay at the defaults — useful enough, but nowhere near what it's capable of. If you treat it as a person, you'll misunderstand what it's doing. If you treat it as an alien species — something genuinely new that deserves to be studied on its own terms — you'll be in a position to actually augment your thinking with it, and to make decisions about how to live with it that are yours, not accidents. That's what this course is for.
